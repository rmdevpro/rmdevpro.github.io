Joshua26 — Conversationally Cognizant AI Ecosystem Gen 2

Joshua26 is the second-generation Conversationally Cognizant AI ecosystem. After building Joshua, the concepts had proven themselves but the software had accumulated the kind of debt that is cheaper to rewrite than repair. Given how quickly LLMs can produce code, and the processes I had developed for building with them, a clean refactor was the obvious path — carry the ideas forward, leave the implementation behind. Joshua26 is that refactor. I do not code directly — I design, direct, and review. Through Joshua and into Joshua26, the building has been done primarily through Claude Code, Gemini CLI, Codex CLI, and Cursor, using MCP servers to call cloud LLMs for the more complex work. With the advent of Hopper — Joshua26's own engineering agent — that is now starting to shift toward the ecosystem's own agents building and maintaining themselves. Architecture documentation, requirements, and design documents are available in the this repository. Code is not included at this time — it will be released in sections. If you would like early access to specific components, please reach out.

Following from the concept papers, Joshua26 is a web of independent software modules called MADs, generally following a microservices pattern, but built to become intelligent autonomous actors as the ecosystem improves. This is a hybrid ecosystem spread across three bare-metal machines and integrated with many cloud services including cloud-hosted GPUs, Google Cloud, and all the tier 1 LLM providers. This includes 16 services across 46 containers and over 50 distinct technologies. This is a research project with a single human participant, not a commercial deployment, and so I focus on discovery and capability over typical enterprise concerns. The repository contains a large number of high-level designs, requirements documents, ADRs, and other artifacts that describe the ecosystem in great depth.

Services

The following are the services currently planned or deployed in the Joshua26 ecosystem. The components consist of a mix of States 1, 2, and 3 MADs, with the target of migrating all to State 3 — where the container reduces to a bootstrap kernel and both AE and TE are dynamically loaded as versioned packages at runtime. Kaiser is the first State 3 pMAD. I use OTS open source components whenever possible, relying heavily on prebuilt Docker images so that I can focus on novel work. Deployed does not mean final. It means functioning. Autonomy and conversational cognition exist only in pockets as of yet, but improve with each passing day.

On the Name

"Joshua, what are you doing?" Science fiction is replete with stories of AI gone wrong where humans ultimately defeat, destroy, outwit, or unplug the machine. There are the more poetic approaches — V'Ger merging with people, Number 5 not wanting to be disassembled, Daystrom appealing to the humanity he encoded in M5 — but these all resolve by teaching the machine to be more human. WarGames, 1983, used none of those tropes. In it, the human-engineered system went wrong, and the military and scientists were thoroughly defeated. The machine was simply smarter and faster than they were. This reflects the current fear many have of AI, tied to our greatest fear of the 1980s and to a certain extent today — nuclear war. "What if the machines got control of the nukes?" But our fears now seem closer to: "Since it will be faster and smarter than us, how do we keep it from destroying us?" I wonder how responsible that movie is for ongoing public fears of AGI. Either way, the film was strangely prescient.

What makes it particularly fantastic is that the problem was not solved by defeating Joshua — it was solved by prompting him, just as we do with LLMs, to discover the answer through his own reinforcement learning cycle. Thousands of games against himself until the foolishness of defeating your enemy became mathematically obvious. This is Sutton's bitter lesson expressed perfectly, 36 years before he wrote the paper. It has been one of my favorite movies since I watched it on our VCR dozens of times when I was 10. The parallels to my own feelings about AI and its approaches made the name both fun and relevant.
