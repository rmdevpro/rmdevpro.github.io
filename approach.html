How I Work

I do not code directly — not because I cannot, but because I choose not to. I have coded extensively over the course of my career, but since August 2025 I have chosen to work, nearly exclusively, through LLMs in one fashion or another. For that matter, I do not write documents either. Nearly nothing on this site or in my repositories was written directly by me. This choice was driven by two goals: discovery and speed. Discovery meant seeing where the real capabilities and holes are when you force yourself not to fall back on traditional engineering and work habits. What actually works when you refuse the familiar shortcuts? Where do the LLMs genuinely deliver, and where do they fall down in ways you would not expect? Speed meant accomplishing in months what would otherwise take years. I did not have time to learn every technology, read every paper, or write the volume of code and documentation this project required — but the LLMs could, under direction.

The primary tools have been Claude Code, Gemini CLI, Codex CLI, and Cursor, using MCP servers to make direct calls to cloud LLMs for the more complex work.

I estimate I have spent more than 1,500 hours in active conversation with LLMs since August 2025. Through that I have come to know them well — their cognitive characters, which models excel at what, where hallucination hides, and the strange ways they fail to follow directions, or fail to see what is obvious, that you cannot predict until you have lived through them. I have learned to work with multiple LLMs from different providers as a matter of course — second and third opinions are not optional when the first opinion is confidently wrong as often as it is.

The learning process has been extraordinary. Ask a question, get a summary, drill further until the concept is genuinely understood, then implement, find what does not work, come back and ask more. That loop, repeated thousands of times, has accelerated both my understanding and my output far beyond what traditional study and development would have produced in the same period. You will notice a lack of academic citation in my work for concepts that experts will find familiar in others' work. Much of the time the concepts I have come up with have converged with others' work, which I take as independent validation. Other times, I am working off of the knowledge presented by the LLMs and citations are therefore not present, highlighting the sorts of questions of intellectual property we face in this age. For those that are curious, I recorded several chats with LLMs in the Joshua26 repository.

In summary I can say this. The LLM is not the tool that much of the industry thinks it is. Even with chain-of-thought loops, I am confident it can never achieve AGI, for a variety of reasons. It is significantly overrated by a large portion of the engineering community, but it is also, in many ways, underrated by narrow views of how to use it. I believe AGI is best thought of as an ensemble learning problem where the LLM plays a key role. LLMs on their own, however, have been genuinely transformative as a component for me in learning, working, and building. The thing that is critical to success is not knowing which questions to ask, but knowing how to ask questions, and knowing when to ask them again of another source.
