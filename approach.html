How I Work

I do not code directly — not because I cannot, but as an intentional decision. I have coded extensively over the course of my career. The choice to work exclusively through LLMs was driven by two goals: discovery and speed. Discovery meant seeing where the real capabilities and holes are when you force yourself not to fall back on traditional engineering habits. What actually works when you refuse the familiar shortcuts? Where do the LLMs genuinely deliver, and where do they fall down in ways you would not expect? Speed meant accomplishing in months what would otherwise take years. I did not have time to learn every technology I needed — but I could ask about them. I did not have time to read everything — but I could ask for summaries and drill deeper. I did not have time to write the volume of code and documentation this project required — but the LLMs could, under direction.

The primary tools have been Claude Code, Gemini CLI, Codex CLI, and Cursor, using MCP servers to make direct calls to cloud LLMs for the more complex work.

I estimate over 1,500 hours of active conversation with LLMs since August 2025. Through that I have come to know them well — their cognitive characters, which models excel at what, where hallucination hides, and the strange ways they fail to follow directions that you would not predict until you have lived through them. I have learned to work with multiple LLMs from different providers as a matter of course — second and third opinions are not optional when the first opinion is confidently wrong as often as it is.

The learning process has been extraordinary. Ask a question, get a summary, drill further until the concept is genuinely understood, then implement, find what does not work, come back and ask more. That loop, repeated thousands of times, has accelerated both my understanding and my output far beyond what traditional study and development would have produced in the same period. The LLM is not the tool that much of the industry thinks it is — it is not a replacement for engineering judgment, and it is significantly overrated by a large portion of the engineering community. But as a component in a learning and building process directed by someone who knows what questions to ask, it is genuinely transformative.
